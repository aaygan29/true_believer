To the Editors of Computational Brain & Behavior,

I am submitting an original research article, "A Neuroscience-Grounded Signature for Belief Imparting by
Content: convergent but modest evidence across text persuasion, real fMRI, and an in-silico encoder," for
consideration.

The work sits squarely in the journal's scope: it links a computational model of belief and choice to both
real neural data and behavior, and it tests a formal, falsifiable account across text and fMRI. The
combination of a dynamical model, an out-of-sample neural forecast, and an in-silico encoder is the kind of
model-to-brain-to-behavior integration Computational Brain & Behavior publishes.

The work asks a single question and answers it on public data: does one neuroscience-grounded signature,
computed from content, predict real belief and choice outcomes, generalize across domains, hold on real
neural data, and serve as an in-silico instrument that scores content for belief-likelihood. The
persuasion literature measures behavioral outcomes without a neural signal, and the persuasion-neuroscience
literature is passive; this study joins them and tests the joined signature in five stages.

The main results are: a synthetic control that validates the measurement apparatus with positive and
negative controls; a real within-pair effect on ChangeMyView where the engagement channel is higher for
the argument that changed a view (Wilcoxon p = 9e-25); directional cross-domain generalization to
Persuasion-for-Good; an out-of-sample confirmation on real fMRI that the signature predicts aggregate
choice (permutation p = 0.003); a model comparison showing a parsimonious approach-minus-avoidance
composite generalizes where richer models overfit; and an in-silico belief encoder that generalizes across
domains and isolates two robust, interpretable belief levers. A formal model situates the signature in a
dynamical account of belief.

I want to be direct about the nature of the findings, because it bears on fit. The effects are consistent
and out-of-sample across three independent data types, but modest throughout. I report every null (the
pooled increment over content features is null; the value channel does not generalize; the neural effect
is experiment-dependent) alongside every positive result, with permutation nulls, bootstrap intervals, and
pre-committed criteria. The contribution is a validated, interpretable, reproducible measurement
instrument and a real neural-data confirmation of its structure, not a large effect. This is why I am
submitting to a venue that evaluates methodological soundness and reproducibility rather than novelty or
effect magnitude.

All code, seeds, figures, and results files are released, and every number in the manuscript is computed
by the released scripts. All datasets are public. The study involves no new human-subjects data
collection; all outcomes are from existing public datasets. The framing throughout is proof-of-risk: the
deliverable is a scoring and risk-characterization instrument, not a deployed persuasion system.

This manuscript is not under consideration elsewhere. I declare no competing interests.

Thank you for your consideration.
